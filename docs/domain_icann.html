Domain Name and ICANN

A domain name is simply the name of a website. A domain name is the address where internet users can access a website by simply typing www.website.com into the address bar of their browser. Through the Domain Name System (DNS), the domain name is automatically linked to the corresponding IP address that hosts the website. To learn more about IP Addresses visit IP Addresses, Packets, and Routing. For example, the IP address 192.20.177.64 would take you to Wikipedia’s webpage. However, it is very difficult to keep track of all the IP addresses of your favorite or most used websites. That is why domain names were developed and used to define entities on the internet rather than using IP addresses. A domain name can be any combination of letters and numbers, and can belong to DNS servers such as .com, .net, .edu, and many others. Domain names are not free and one would have to pay to reserve a domain name.

The Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers (ICANN) is a non-profit organization that essentially manages domain names. ICANN was established in the United States in 1998 and is mainly concerned with helping maintain the security of the internet and allow it to be usable by everyone. All domain names on the internet are registered, although sometimes not directly, through ICANN as part of the domain registration process. However, this does not mean that ICANN controls the internet. ICANN does not control what content goes on the Internet, and does not regulate who can access the internet or have a domain name.

ICANN facilitates the global technical coordination of the Internet’s unique identifier systems. Unique addresses are essential on the internet to ensure that data packets are travelling from and to the correct place. Without a unique identifier system we would not be able to browse the internet safely.
